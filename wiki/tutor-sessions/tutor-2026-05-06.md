### Q4 — Lecture 1: Bayes refresher + MLE (13:00)

**Question / instruction:** *Lecture 1 of the Phase-1 (lecture mode) sequence. Bayes' rule refresher front-loaded, then MLE in full. Topic 6 — statistical inference. Concept-page-style render in [[tutor-2026-05-06-live]]; mirrored here for the chronological log so the content survives the next live-file overwrite.*

**Answer / dialogue:**

#### Why Bayes first

MAP and significance testing are both Bayes-rule applications wearing different costumes. MLE is the no-prior version of MAP. If Bayes is sharp, three of the four blank topics collapse into "MLE plus a twist." 15 min on Bayes saves an hour later.

#### Part 1 — Bayes' rule refresher

**Example first — medical-test classic.** Test 99% accurate (both directions), disease prevalence 1 in 1000, your test is positive. Naive intuition: "$\approx 99\%$." Bayes: **about 9%**.

Let $D = $ "have disease," $T = $ "test positive." Given $P(D) = 0.001$, $P(T \mid D) = 0.99$, $P(T \mid D^c) = 0.01$.

$$P(D \mid T) = \frac{P(T \mid D)\,P(D)}{P(T)} = \frac{0.99 \cdot 0.001}{0.99 \cdot 0.001 + 0.01 \cdot 0.999} = \frac{0.00099}{0.01098} \approx 9.0\%$$

Lesson: when prior is small, even a strong likelihood doesn't push the posterior near 1. Base rate dominates. Why doctors order a second test.

**Framework — three named pieces, one identity.**

| Symbol | Name | What it is | Medical example |
|---|---|---|---|
| $P(\theta)$ or $\pi(\theta)$ | **Prior** | Belief about $\theta$ *before* data | $P(D) = 0.001$ |
| $P(x \mid \theta)$ | **Likelihood** | How data $x$ is distributed *given* $\theta$ | $P(T \mid D) = 0.99$ |
| $P(\theta \mid x)$ | **Posterior** | Belief about $\theta$ *after* data | $P(D \mid T) \approx 9\%$ |

$$\boxed{\,P(\theta \mid x) = \frac{P(x \mid \theta)\,P(\theta)}{P(x)}\,}$$

"Posterior = likelihood times prior, divided by evidence." Denominator is the **evidence** / marginal likelihood — same number for every $\theta$, just normalizes. Compute unnormalized posterior first, normalize at the end.

**Discrete vs continuous $\theta$.** Same machinery, two flavors of output:
- **Discrete $\theta$** $\to$ posterior PMF. Pick max-posterior $\theta$ = **MAP detection**.
- **Continuous $\theta$** $\to$ posterior PDF. Pick max-posterior $\theta$ = **MAP estimation**. Posterior mean = **LMS / MMSE estimation**.

> [!note] Naming spaghetti
> - **MAP** = "maximum a posteriori" = pick $\theta$ with highest posterior. Discrete or continuous.
> - **LMS** in the wiki = "least mean square" = posterior mean = $E[\theta \mid X]$. Minimizes MSE.
> - **LMSE** in HW7 / final = "linear MMSE" = the linear version $\hat\theta = aX + b$. Different beast.

**The "which conditional?" gotcha.** $P(x \mid \theta)$ and $P(\theta \mid x)$ read identically but mean opposite things.

| Symbol | Reads | Direction | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| $P(x \mid \theta)$ | "Prob of data given parameter" | **Forward** — build from model | $P(T \mid D) = 0.99$ |
| $P(\theta \mid x)$ | "Prob of parameter given data" | **Reverse** — what you want | $P(D \mid T) \approx 9\%$ |

Bayes' rule is literally the gear that flips one into the other. Common corruption: **prosecutor's fallacy** — confusing "prob of evidence given innocence" with "prob of innocence given evidence."

**What to internalize for Bayes.**

| Memorize cold | Derive on the spot |
|---|---|
| Identity $P(\theta\mid x) = P(x\mid\theta)P(\theta)/P(x)$ | Denominator $P(x)$ via total probability |
| Three names — prior, likelihood, posterior | The specific numbers from the problem |
| The flip needs both prior and evidence | When MAP vs LMS — depends on loss function |

> [!tip] Self-check
> Friend's COVID test positive, sensitivity 95%, specificity 95%, base rate 5%. Trust it?
> $$P(\text{COVID} \mid +) = \frac{0.95 \cdot 0.05}{0.95 \cdot 0.05 + 0.05 \cdot 0.95} = \frac{0.0475}{0.095} = 50\%$$
> Half. Test result and base rate equally informative when equally calibrated.

#### Part 2 — Maximum Likelihood Estimation

**Example first — coin-flip MLE.** Flip 10 times, observe 7H/3T. No prior on $p$. MLE asks: *"Which $p$ makes my observed data most likely?"*

$$L(p) = \binom{10}{7} p^7 (1-p)^3$$

$\binom{10}{7}$ is constant in $p$ — drop during optimization. Take log:

$$\ell(p) = \log\binom{10}{7} + 7\log p + 3\log(1-p)$$

Differentiate, set to zero:

$$\frac{d\ell}{dp} = \frac{7}{p} - \frac{3}{1-p} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; 7(1-p) = 3p \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{\hat p_{\text{MLE}} = 0.7}$$

Sample proportion — that's not coincidence, it's the structural answer for Bernoulli MLE.

**Framework — three steps, every time:**

1. **Write the likelihood** $L(\theta) = p(x \mid \theta)$ — joint pdf/pmf as function of $\theta$ for **fixed** observed data.
2. **Take the log** $\ell(\theta) = \log L(\theta)$ — products $\to$ sums.
3. **Maximize** — set $d\ell/d\theta = 0$ and solve. (For boundary cases like $\text{Uniform}(0, \theta)$, just stare at the likelihood.)

For i.i.d. data:

$$L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n p(x_i \mid \theta) \;\Rightarrow\; \ell(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \log p(x_i \mid \theta)$$

**Four MLEs to recognize on sight.**

| Distribution | Parameter | MLE | Mnemonic |
|---|---|---|---|
| $\text{Bernoulli}(p)$ | $p$ | $\hat p = \bar x$ | "fraction of 1s" |
| $\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$ | $\lambda$ | $\hat\lambda = \bar x$ | "average count" |
| $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, $\sigma^2$ known | $\mu$ | $\hat\mu = \bar x$ | "sample mean" |
| $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, both unknown | $\sigma^2$ | $\hat\sigma^2 = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum(x_i - \bar x)^2$ | "biased — divides by $n$" |
| $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$ | $\lambda$ | $\hat\lambda = 1/\bar x$ | "inverse mean wait" |
| $\text{Uniform}(0, \theta)$ | $\theta$ | $\hat\theta = \max(x_i)$ | "boundary case" |

For one-parameter exponential-family distributions, MLE is almost always **a function of the sample mean**.

**Full Gaussian-mean derivation.** $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ i.i.d. $\sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, $\sigma^2$ known.

$$L(\mu) = \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

$$\ell(\mu) = -\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu)^2$$

$$\frac{d\ell}{d\mu} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu) = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{\hat\mu_{\text{MLE}} = \bar x}$$

**$\sigma^2$ drops out** — the answer doesn't depend on noise variance (in this one-parameter problem). Common slip: students think $\sigma^2$ should appear; it doesn't.

> [!warning] Two failure modes on this derivation
> 1. Forgetting to drop the $-\tfrac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2)$ constant in $\mu$ — its derivative is zero.
> 2. Sign error: $\dfrac{d}{d\mu}(x_i - \mu)^2 = -2(x_i - \mu)$. The $(-1)$ from $d/d\mu(x_i - \mu)$.

**Why log? Three reasons.** (1) Algebra — products $\to$ sums. (2) Numerics — $L(\theta)$ for $n=100$ is product of 100 numbers in $[0,1]$, underflows to 0. (3) Monotonicity — $\arg\max$ is preserved.

**MLE vs MAP — the one-line difference.**

$$\hat\theta_{\text{MLE}} = \arg\max_\theta p(x \mid \theta) \qquad \hat\theta_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta p(x \mid \theta)\,p(\theta)$$

**MLE = MAP with flat prior.** When the prior is constant in $\theta$, multiplying by it doesn't change the max location. MLE is the special case where you say "no prior info, every $\theta$ equally likely a priori."

**What to internalize for MLE.**

| Memorize cold | Derive on the spot | Look up |
|---|---|---|
| Three-step recipe | Specific MLEs not in the table | Combinatorial factors |
| Bernoulli/Poisson/Gaussian-mean = $\bar x$ | Gaussian-variance MLE | Distribution PDFs/PMFs |
| MLE = MAP with flat prior | MLE bias check | |

**Common mistakes flagged:**

1. **Confusing $p(x\mid\theta)$ with $p(\theta\mid x)$.** Likelihood = forward; posterior = reverse. MLE works on the likelihood — no Bayes flip because no prior.
2. **Dropping the wrong constants.** Constants in $\theta$ get dropped; everything depending on $\theta$ stays.
3. **MLE not unbiased.** $E[\hat\sigma^2_{\text{MLE}}] = \tfrac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2 < \sigma^2$. Unbiased version uses $1/(n-1)$ — different estimator.
4. **Boundary cases.** $\text{Uniform}(0, \theta)$: $\hat\theta = \max(x_i)$, by inspection, not derivative.

### Q5 — Marginally Gaussian vs jointly Gaussian (13:15)

**Question:** *"can you explain this please Marginally Gaussian ≠ jointly Gaussian. Two RVs can both be Gaussian on the margin but have a non-Gaussian joint."*

**Answer / dialogue:**

#### The canonical counterexample (concrete, no hand-waving)

Let $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. Independently, let $Z$ be a coin flip: $P(Z = +1) = P(Z = -1) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, with $Z \perp X$. Define

$$Y = ZX$$

**Claim 1 — $Y$ is also $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$.** Standard Gaussian is symmetric: $-X \stackrel{d}{=} X$ (same distribution). So conditional on $Z = +1$, $Y = X \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$; conditional on $Z = -1$, $Y = -X \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Mixing a half-half blend of two identical distributions gives back the same distribution: $Y \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$.

**Claim 2 — both marginals are standard Gaussian.** $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ by construction; $Y \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ by Claim 1. Each viewed alone, indistinguishable from i.i.d. standard normals.

**Claim 3 — the joint $(X, Y)$ is NOT bivariate Gaussian.** $Y = ZX$ means $Y = +X$ or $Y = -X$. The support of the joint is the **union of the two diagonal lines $y = x$ and $y = -x$** — a set of measure zero in $\mathbb{R}^2$. A genuine bivariate Gaussian has full elliptical support in the plane (positive density everywhere, contours are ellipses). This isn't even close.

**Claim 4 — covariance is zero.**

$$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y] = E[ZX^2] - 0 = E[Z]\,E[X^2] = 0 \cdot 1 = 0$$

(using $Z \perp X$ so $E[ZX^2] = E[Z]E[X^2]$, and $E[Z] = 0$). So **uncorrelated**.

**Claim 5 — but $X$ and $Y$ are wildly dependent.** $|Y| = |Z||X| = |X|$ always. Knowing $X = 2$ tells you $Y \in \{-2, +2\}$ — only two possible values, not a full Gaussian's worth.

So we have: marginals both Gaussian, $\text{Cov} = 0$, but the pair is **not** independent and **not** jointly Gaussian.

#### Tie-back to your Q1 mistake

Q1's gotcha was "$\text{Cov} = 0 \neq$ independent" — same trap, dressed up. The bivariate-Gaussian world is the **one place** where uncorrelated DOES imply independent. Outside that world, you can't pull that inference. **This is the answer to "why is bivariate Gaussian special."** Drop the "jointly" qualifier and the implication breaks.

#### Framework — three building blocks

1. **Marginal distribution.** Projection of the joint onto one axis: $f_X(x) = \int f_{X,Y}(x, y)\,dy$. Throws away dependence info.
2. **Joint distribution.** The full multivariate density — carries the dependence structure.
3. **Bivariate Gaussian.** A specific joint shape: elliptical contours, fully characterized by mean vector + covariance matrix. Marginals being Gaussian is **necessary but not sufficient** for the joint to be bivariate Gaussian.

#### Formal statement

$X, Y$ jointly Gaussian $\Rightarrow$ marginals Gaussian. **The converse fails.** Counterexample exists ($Y = ZX$ above). Equivalent statement: knowing each marginal does NOT determine the joint.

#### Gotchas — what to watch for on the exam

- "Both marginals Gaussian" alone does NOT let you use bivariate-Gaussian formulas:
   - The conditional-expectation linear form $E[Y \mid X = x] = \mu_Y + \rho\frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}(x - \mu_X)$.
   - The conditional-variance constancy $\text{Var}(Y \mid X) = \sigma_Y^2(1 - \rho^2)$.
   - The LMSE = MMSE collapse (next).
- **If a problem says "$X, Y$ are both Gaussian"** without saying "**jointly** Gaussian," watch out — you can't assume bivariate Gaussian.
- **If it says "$(X, Y)$ is bivariate Gaussian" or "jointly Gaussian,"** you can.
- **The famous LMSE-equals-MMSE result** ($\hat{X}_{\text{LMSE}} = \hat{X}_{\text{MMSE}}$) requires JOINT Gaussianity, not just marginal — directly relevant to LMSE topic 6 next.

#### Section 0 — Connect back to Lecture 1

MAP builds on the Bayes refresher in Lecture 1 — same posterior, but instead of just *computing* the posterior, we now **pick the value of $\theta$ that maximizes it**. That single extra step is all "MAP" is.

| | Lecture 1 (Bayes) | Lecture 2 (MAP) |
|---|---|---|
| Output | Full posterior $p(\theta\mid x)$ | A single $\hat\theta$ |
| Operation | Compute Bayes' rule | Bayes' rule, then $\arg\max$ over $\theta$ |
| Used when | You want to report uncertainty | You have to commit to one decision |

#### Section 1 — Worked example: antipodal signaling over AWGN

**Setup.** Binary symbol $\theta\in\{+1,-1\}$ over $X = \theta + N$, $N\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$. Priors $P(\theta=+1)=\pi_0$, $P(\theta=-1)=\pi_1$. Numbers: $\sigma^2=1$, $\pi_0=0.7$, $\pi_1=0.3$, observed $x=0.4$.

**Step 1 — Identify the four pieces.** Parameter $\theta$ (discrete $\to$ MAP detection); prior $\pi_0=0.7,\pi_1=0.3$; likelihood $p(x\mid\theta)\sim\mathcal{N}(\theta,\sigma^2)$; observation $x=0.4$. **Recognition cue:** the word "prior" in the problem $\to$ MAP.

**Step 2 — Posterior $\propto$ likelihood $\times$ prior.** Don't bother with $p(x)$ — same in both branches.
$$P(+1\mid x) \propto \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\!\left(-\tfrac{(x-1)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)\pi_0$$
$$P(-1\mid x) \propto \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\!\left(-\tfrac{(x+1)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)\pi_1$$

**Step 3 — Likelihood ratio test (binary form).**
$$\frac{p(x\mid+1)}{p(x\mid-1)} \;\overset{+1}{\underset{-1}{\gtrless}}\; \frac{\pi_1}{\pi_0}$$

**Watch the ratio direction:** $H_1$ on top of likelihood, $\pi_0$ on top of threshold — they are **flipped**. Take $\log$:
$$\log\frac{p(x\mid+1)}{p(x\mid-1)} = \frac{(x+1)^2 - (x-1)^2}{2\sigma^2} = \frac{2x}{\sigma^2}$$

So the rule reduces to:
$$x \;\overset{+1}{\underset{-1}{\gtrless}}\; \tau, \qquad \tau = \frac{\sigma^2}{2}\log\frac{\pi_1}{\pi_0}$$

**Step 4 — Numbers.** $\tau = (1/2)\log(0.3/0.7) \approx -0.424$. Observation $x=0.4 > -0.424$, so $\hat\theta_{\text{MAP}} = +1$. Sanity-check: prior favors $+1$, threshold shifts toward less-likely $-1$ (negative). Correct direction.

> [!example] Equal-prior special case
> If $\pi_0=\pi_1=0.5$, then $\tau=0$ — the **classic sign detector**. MAP collapses to MLE.

#### Section 2 — Framework (5 building blocks)

1. **Posterior $\propto$ likelihood $\times$ prior.** Drop normalizer $p(x)$ for argmax.
2. **Argmax over $\theta$.** Discrete: compare unnormalized posteriors. Continuous: $\log$-derivative-zero.
3. **Binary form: likelihood ratio test.** $\Lambda(x) = p(x\mid H_1)/p(x\mid H_0) \;\overset{H_1}{\underset{H_0}{\gtrless}}\; P(H_0)/P(H_1)$. Ratio direction flipped.
4. **MAP $=$ MLE under flat prior.** $P(\theta)=$ const drops out of argmax.
5. **Log-domain trick.** Products $\to$ sums, Gaussian $\to$ quadratic, argmax preserved.

#### Section 3 — Second example: binary symmetric channel with Bernoulli prior

**Setup.** Bit $\theta\in\{0,1\}$, channel flips with $p=0.1$, prior $P(\theta=1)=0.2$, $P(\theta=0)=0.8$, observe $X=1$.

**Unnormalized posteriors:**
$$P(\theta=1\mid X=1) \propto 0.9 \cdot 0.2 = 0.18$$
$$P(\theta=0\mid X=1) \propto 0.1 \cdot 0.8 = 0.08$$

**Argmax:** $0.18 > 0.08 \Rightarrow \hat\theta_{\text{MAP}}=1$.

**Compare to MLE:** likelihood ratio $0.9/0.1=9$ overpowers prior ratio $0.2/0.8=0.25$, so MLE also picks $\theta=1$. **They agree here.** But if the channel were $p=0.4$: MAP picks $\theta=0$ (prior-dominated), MLE picks $\theta=1$ (likelihood-only). That divergence is when MAP earns its keep.

**Posterior probability:** $P(\theta=1\mid X=1) = 0.18/0.26 \approx 0.692$ — only ~69% sure even after seeing $X=1$ because the prior was so strongly against it. Same Bayes' rule arithmetic as the medical-test example from Lecture 1.

#### Section 4 — Formal definition

Generic:
$$\hat\theta_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta P(\theta\mid x) = \arg\max_\theta\bigl[p(x\mid\theta)\,P(\theta)\bigr]$$

Binary LRT form:
$$\frac{p(x\mid H_1)}{p(x\mid H_0)} > \frac{P(H_0)}{P(H_1)} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{decide } H_1$$

Continuous estimation (with prior density $p(\theta)$):
$$\hat\theta_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta\bigl[\log p(x\mid\theta) + \log p(\theta)\bigr]$$

#### Section 5 — When to use it (recognition cues)

Three flavors on the EEE 350 final:
1. Signal detection over noisy channel with known priors (Section 1 shape).
2. Classification with non-uniform class priors (Section 3 shape).
3. Continuous parameter estimation with a prior density.

**Trigger word:** "**prior**" $\to$ MAP. No prior or "estimate from data" only $\to$ MLE. "Equally likely" / $P(H_0)=P(H_1)=0.5$ $\to$ MAP $=$ MLE.

#### Section 6 — Internalize vs memorize

| Memorize cold | Look up on cheat sheet | Derive on the spot |
|---|---|---|
| Recognition cue: "prior" $\to$ MAP | Exact LRT form + ratio direction | Log-likelihood ratio for Gaussian channel |
| 5-block framework | Gaussian / Bernoulli PDFs | Threshold $\tau$ for antipodal-AWGN problem |
| MAP $=$ MLE when priors equal | Gaussian-Gaussian conjugate posterior | Numerical answer once $\tau$ known |

**Memorize NOTHING from MAP itself as a formula** — the $\arg\max$ rule and LRT are on the cheat sheet. Recognize the *structure*: see "prior" $\to$ open MAP page.

#### Section 7 — Gotchas (4 sharp ones)

1. **Likelihood-ratio direction.** $\Lambda$ has $H_1$ on top; threshold has $P(H_0)$ on top — **flipped**. Most common exam slip. Re-derive from $p(x\mid H_1)P(H_1) > p(x\mid H_0)P(H_0)$ if you forget.
2. **MAP collapses to MLE under equal priors.** Don't carry the $\log(\pi_1/\pi_0)=0$ term through; zero out the threshold immediately.
3. **MAP maximizes the *posterior*, not the *likelihood*.** Forgetting the prior silently switches you back to MLE.
4. **Continuous-$\theta$ MAP needs the $+\log p(\theta)$ term in the derivative.** Same calculus as MLE but with extra prior contribution. Drop it $\to$ silently MLE.
